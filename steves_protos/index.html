Will there be another housing crisis?

What caused the last housing crisis?

Mid 2005, the mortgage market began suffering serious problems. Where "seriously delinquent" (90 days or more past due or in the process of foreclosure) mortgages averaged 1.7% from 1979 to 2006, by the second quarter of 2008 the number had surged to 4.5%. This trend foreshadowed a sharp rise in foreclosures: roughly 1.2 million foreclosures were started in the first half of 2008, an increase of 79% from the 650,000 in the first half of 2007 (Federal Reserve estimates). Within these foreclosures there was a particularly large concentration of defaults that were classified as "subprime" or "near-prime" mortgages--often these were classified as "non-prime" mortgages.

Although the categories are not rigidly defined, non-prime loans are generally targeted to borrowers who have tarnished credit histories and little savings available for down payments. These types of mortgages are often made to borrowers with more minor credit quality issues or borrowers who are unable or unwilling to provide full documentation of assets or income; some of these borrowers are investing in real estate rather than occupying the properties they purchase. As the number of subprime loans nearly doubled from 2003 to 2005 (from 1.1 million in 2003 to 1.9 million in 2005), the share of these loans that were short-term hybrids grew as well to roughly 80 percent of all non-prime loans originated in 2005—or more than 1.5 million mortgages with teaser rates that would expire in 2007 and 2008. Despite these concerns, lending to risky borrowers grew rapidly in the 2000s.

If the risk of subprime mortgages were not enough, non-prime mortgages were bundled as "securities" that were then sold to investors. As subprime lending grew, so too did the extent to which these loans were pooled into securities and sold to investors. This process, known as securitization, transforms illiquid individual mortgages into financial products that can be bought and sold as widely as stocks and bonds.

For more information on the above, see "The Rise in Mortgage Defaults" by Mayer, Pence, and Sherlund (2008).

In short, the perfect storm had arrived:

  • An increase in risky subprime loans
  • These loans carried teaser rates that both expired quickly and were obfuscated from consumers
  • Predatory lending practices against non-sophisticated buyers
  • Bundling these already risky products into securities, which were then taken to the broader market
With all of the above in hand, the world economy witnessed not only a housing crisis, but also a global recession.

What is the historical trend of housing sales?

While the last housing crisis crashed the market, it did eventually recover. From the chart below, you can get a good and simple view of how (since 1991) the picture of house sales has evolved and changed. The data represents a 27-year view, using the FHFA House Price Index (HPI).

The HPI is a broad measure of the movement of single-family house prices and is a weighted, repeat-sales index, meaning that it measures average price changes in repeat sales or refinancings on the same properties. This information is obtained by reviewing repeat mortgage transactions on single-family properties whose mortgages have been purchased or securitized by Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac since January 1975. The HPI serves as a timely, accurate indicator of house price trends at various geographic levels. Because of the breadth of the sample, it provides more information than is available in other house price indexes. It also provides housing economists with an improved analytical tool that is useful for estimating changes in the rates of mortgage defaults, prepayments and housing affordability in specific geographic areas.

For more information on the HPI, see the FHFA site.

You can view the seasonally adjusted or non-adjusted numbers and filter by date range using the slider to explore how the HPI has changed over the last 27 years.



What's interesting about the HPI is that just prior to the housing crisis in 2008, the peak HPI was 226. Now, in 2018 the HPI is 266. For perspective, according to the US Census the median income in 2007 was $50, 233.00. At the end of 2017, it was $61,372.00. The average house price in 2008 was $292,600.00, and in 2018 it's $377,200.00.

What are some demographic changes that can impact housing?

Different demographic trends, such as age, population and income, can impact the housing market in different ways. Age can impact housing in a number of ways with some demographic profiles having more debt and/or credit power. We are seeing historic highs for student debt, for example. Further, trends such as population increases or decreases can drive demand for housing up or down, thus driving prices up or down. Income levels and credit habits across age groups is also an important factor; younger people tend to have less disposable income, which impacts their ability to purchase houses. More senior demographics may choose to either not move or sell and move into senior housing.



Baby boomers are a particularly large group of the American population, so one needs to watch this demographic group very carefully. Large-scale shifts with this group could impact housing prices.

According to SeniorLiving.org "...Baby Boomers—those Americans born between 1946 and 1964—turns 65. From now until 2030, 10,000 Baby Boomers each day will hit retirement age."

In the next 10 to 15 years well over 3.5 million people will be reaching their retirement age every year. This is a rate that is estimated to be 1 to 1.5 million higher than historical figures. As these retirees move out of their primary residence and their primary housing units are put on sale, the market will see a roughly 30% to 50% more units than historical figure.
